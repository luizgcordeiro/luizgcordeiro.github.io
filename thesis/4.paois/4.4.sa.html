4.4. Steinberg algebras

\subsection{Definitions and notation} Steinberg algebras were independently introduced in \cite{MR2565546} and \cite{MR3274831}, as algebraic analogues of groupoid C*-algebras, and are generalizations of Leavitt path algebras and universal inverse semigroup algebras. We refer to \cite{MR3743184} and \cite{carlsenrout2017} for more details. In this section, $R$ is a fixed commutative ring with unit. Given an ample Hausdorff groupoid $\G$, we denote by $R^{\G}$ the $R$-module of $R$-valued functions on $\G$. Given $A\subseteq\G$, we define $1_A$ as the characteristic function of $A$ (with values in $R$).\nomenclature[Z]{$1_A$}{Characteristic function of a set $A$} \begin{definition}[{\cite[Definition 4.1]{MR2565546}}]\nomenclature[Z]{$A_R(\G)$}{Steinberg algebra}\index{Steinberg algebra} Given an ample Hausdorff groupoid $\G$, $A_R(\G)$ is the $R$-submodule of $R^{\G}$ generated by the characteristic functions of compact-open bisections of $\G$. \end{definition} The \emph{support} of $f\in R^{\G}$ is defined as $\supp(f)=\overline{\left\{a\in\G:f(a)\neq 0\right\}}$. \begin{proposition}[{\cite[Lemma 3.3.]{MR3274831}}] If $\G$ is an ample Hausdorff groupoid, then $A_R(\G)$ coincides with the $R$-module of locally constant compactly supported $R$-valued functions on $\G$. Every $f\in A_R(\G)$ can be written as a finite linear combination \[f=\sum_{i=1}^n r_i1_{A_i}\] where $r_i\in R$ and $\left\{A_i:i=1,\ldots,n\right\}$ is a family of pairwise disjoint compact-open bisections of $\G$, and $\supp(f)=\left\{a\in G:f(a)\neq 0\right\}$. \end{proposition} Recall that $\KB(\G)$ is the semigroup of compact-open bisections of $\G$ (Definition \ref{definitionkbg}). \begin{proof} Since $\G$ is Hausdorff, then compact-open bisections are clopen and their characteristic functions are locally constant. The same is true of their linear combinations. Conversely, if $f$ is a locally constant, compactly supported function on $\G$, $f^{-1}(0)$ is clopen, hence $\supp(f)=\overline{\G\setminus f^{-1}(0)}=\G\setminus f^{-1}(0)$ is clopen as well. Using compactness of $\supp(f)$ and since $\KB(\G)$ is a basis for the topology of $\G$, we can find $A_1,\ldots,A_n\in\KB(\G)$ and $r_1,\ldots,r_n$ such that $\supp(f)=\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i$ and $f=r_i$ on $A_i$. Substituting $A_j$ by $A_j\setminus\bigcup_{i=1}^{j-1}A_i$ for all $j\geq 2$, if necessary, the family $\left\{A_i:i=1,\ldots,n\right\}$ forms a partition of $\supp(f)$, and the equality \[f=\sum_{i=1}^n r_i1_{A_i}\] holds. In particular, $f$ is a linear combination of characteristic functions of compact-open bisections, and therefore $f\in A_R(\G)$.\qedhere \end{proof} \begin{remarkn} Endowing $R$ with the discrete topology, $A_R(\G)$ coincides with the set of compactly supported functions from $\G$ to $R$. \end{remarkn} For every $f\in A_R(\G)$ ($\G$ ample Hausdorff) and every $x\in\G[0]$, $\supp(f)\cap\G_x$ is compact in the discrete space $\G_x$, and hence finite. Similarly, $\supp(f)\cap \G^x$ is finite, and so we can define the \emph{convolution product} \[(fg)(a)=\sum_{xy=a}f(x)g(y)=\sum_{c\in\G^{\ra(a)}}f(c)g(c^{-1}a)=\sum_{d\in\G_{\so(a)}}f(ad^{-1})g(d).\ntag\label{equationconvolutionproductsteinbergalgebra}\] With this product, $A_R(\G)$ is an associative algebra. \begin{definition}[{\cite{MR2565546,MR3274831}}]\index{Steinberg algebra} The $R$-module $A_R(\G)$, endowed with the convolution product of Equation \ref{equationconvolutionproductsteinbergalgebra} is called the \emph{Steinberg algebra} $\G$ (or $(\G,R)$, to make $R$ explicit). \end{definition} From the convolution formula we obtain, for all $f,g\in A_R(\G)$. \[\supp(fg)\subseteq\left\{ab:(a,b)\in(\supp(f)\times\supp(g))\cap\G[2]\right\}=\supp(f)\supp(g).\] We will now prove a universal property for Steinberg algebras, and for this we need the notion of a \emph{Boolean representation}. These are the semigroup morphisms of a Boolean inverse semigroup $S$ into the multiplicative semigroup of an algebra $B$ which respect the order structure of $S$. \begin{definition}[{\cite[Definition 3.9]{MR3274831}}]\label{definitionbooleanrepresentation} A \emph{Boolean representation} of a Boolean inverse semigroup $S$ on an algebra $B$ is a map morphism $\pi:S\to B$ of $S$ in the multiplicative semigroup of $B$ which satisfies \[\pi(s\lor t)=\pi(s)+\pi(t)\]\ whenever $s$ and $t$ are compatible and $s\land t=0$. \end{definition} \begin{example} If $\G$ is an ample Hausdorff groupoid, then the map $\KB(\G)\to A_R(\G)$, $U\mapsto 1_U$, is a Boolean representation of $\KB(\G)$. \end{example} This notion of Boolean representation coincides with \cite[Definition 3.1]{MR3548134} -- except for the fact that we do not assume that the algebra is involutive -- as we prove below: \begin{proposition} A semigroup morphism $\pi:S\to B$ from a Boolean inverse semigroup $S$ to the multiplicative semigroup of an algebra $B$ is a Boolean representation if and only if \begin{enumerate} \item[(i)] $\pi(0)=0$; \item[(ii)] $\pi(s\lor t)=\pi(s)+\pi(t)-\pi(s\land t)$ whenever $s,t\in S$ are compatible; \end{enumerate} (Recall from \ref{propositioncompatibility}(c) that if $s$ and $t$ are compatible in $S$ then $s\land t=st^*t$.) \end{proposition} \begin{proof} Of course if $\pi$ satisfies (i) and (ii) then $\pi$ is a Boolean representation, so we prove the converse direction. Assume $\pi$ is a Boolean representation. Then \[\pi(0)=\pi(0\lor 0)=\pi(0)+\pi(0)\] so $\pi(0)=0$. If $s,t\in S$ are compatible, then $s\land(t\setminus s)=0$, so \[\pi(s\lor t)=\pi(s\lor(t\setminus s))=\pi(s)+\pi(t\setminus s)\] But since $t=(t\land s)\lor(t\setminus s)$, then \[\pi(t)=\pi(s\land t)+\pi(t\setminus s)\] and Property (ii) follows.\qedhere \end{proof} Now we describe the universal property of $A_R(\G)$, which was given in the case of complex algebras in \cite[Theorem~3.10]{MR3274831}. Essentially the same proof works in the case of a general commutative unital ring $R$. \begin{theorem}\label{theoremuniversalpropertysteinberg} Let $R$ be a commutative ring with unit, and $\G$ an ample Hausdorff groupoid. Then $A_R(\G)$ is universal for Boolean representations of $\KB(\G)$, i.e., if $\pi:\KB(\G)\to B$ is a Boolean representation of $\KB(\G)$ in an $R$-algebra $B$, there exists a unique $R$-algebra morphism $\Phi: A_R(\G)\to B$ such that $\Phi(1_U)=\pi(U)$ for all $U\in\KB(\G)$. \end{theorem} \begin{proof} Uniqueness of such $\Phi$ is immediate as $A_R(\G)$ is generated by the family $\left\{1_U:U\in\KB(\G)\right\}$. Given $f\in A_R(\G)$, write $f$ as a linear combination $f=\sum_{i=1}^n r_i1_{U_i}$ for certain $r_i\in R$ and a pairwise disjoint collection of compact-open bisections $U_i\in\KB(\G)$. Define $\Phi: A_R(\G)\to B$ by \[\Phi(f)=\Phi\left(\sum_{i=1}^n r_i1_{U_i}\right)=\sum_{i=1}^n r_i\pi(U_i).\] We first need to prove that $\Phi$ does not depend on the choice of $r_i$ and $U_i$. Suppose $\sum_{i=1}^n r_i1_{U_i}=\sum_{j=1}^m s_j1_{V_j}$, where $s_j\in R$ and $\left\{V_j\right\}_j$ is a pairwise disjoint collection in $\KB(\G)$. Then $\sum_{i,j}r_i1_{U_i\cap V_j}=f=\sum_{i,j}s_j 1_{U_i\cap V_j}$. First of all, let us prove that for all pair of indices $i,j$, we have \[r_i\pi(U_i\cap V_j)=s_j\pi(U_i\cap V_j)\ntag\label{equationuniversalpropertyofbooleanrepresentationsforarg}.\] There are two possibilities: \begin{description} \item[Case 1:] $U_i\cap V_j=\varnothing$. Then $\pi(U_i\cap V_j)=0$ so Equation (\ref{equationuniversalpropertyofbooleanrepresentationsforarg}) is trivially valid. \item[Case 2:] $U_i\cap V_j\neq\varnothing$. In this case, choose $a\in U_i\cap V_j$. Then $r_i=f(a)=s_j$, so of course $r_i\pi(U_i\cap V_j)=s_j\pi(U_i\cap V_j)$. \end{description} We can partition each $U_i$ as $U_i=\bigcup_j (U_i\cap V_j)$, and similarly we can partition each $V_j$ as $V_j=\bigcup_i (U_i\cap V_j)$, so using Equation (\ref{equationuniversalpropertyofbooleanrepresentationsforarg}) and the properties of Boolean representations, \[\sum_{i=1}^n r_i\pi(U_i)=\sum_{i=1}^n\sum_{j=1}^m r_i\pi(U_i\cap V_j)=\sum_{j=1}^m\sum_{i=1}^n s_j\pi(U_i\cap V_j)=\sum_{j=1}^m s_j\pi(V_j)\] therefore $\Phi$ is well-defined. In order to prove that $\Phi$ is an $R$-module morphism, we apply an argument similar to the one above: If $f,g\in A_R(\G)$ have representations $f=\sum_{i=1}^n r_i 1_{A_i}$ and $g=\sum_{j=1}^m s_j 1_{B_j}$ as above, we can, if necessary, add terms of the form $0\cdot 1_{B_j\setminus\supp(f)}$ to the representation of $f$, and similarly for $g$, and assume that $\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i=\bigcup_{j=1}^m B_j$. Therefore we may rewrite, for all $\lambda\in R$, \[f=\sum_{i,j}r_i1_{A_i\cap B_j},\qquad g=\sum_{i,j}s_j1_{A_i\cap B_j},\qquad\text{and}\quad(f+\lambda g)=\sum_{i,j}(r_i+\lambda s_j)1_{A_i\cap B_j},\] and the definition of $\Phi$ readily implies $\Phi(f+\lambda g)=\Phi(f)+\lambda\Phi(g)$, %and we can use these descriptions to calculate $\Phi$: %\begin{align*} %\Phi(f+\lambda g)&=\sum_{i,j}(r_i+\lambda s_j)\pi(A_i\cap B_j)=\left(\sum_{i,j}r_i\pi(A_i\cap B_j)\right)+\lambda\left(\sum_{i,j}s_j\pi(A_i\cap B_j)\right)\\ %&=\Phi(f)+\lambda\Phi(g) %\end{align*} so $\Phi$ is an $R$-module morphism. If $U,V\in\KB(\G)$, then \[\Phi(1_U1_V)=\Phi(1_{UV})=\pi(UV)=\pi(U)\pi(V)=\Phi(1_U)\Phi(1_V),\] and since $\left\{1_U:U\in\KB(\G)\right\}$ generates $A_R(\G)$ then $\Phi$ is an algebra morphism.\qedhere \end{proof} \begin{definition}\label{definitiondiagonalsteinbergalgebra}\index{Diagonal subalgebra} The \emph{diagonal subalgebra} of $A_R(\G)$ is \[D_R(\G)=\left\{f\in A_R(\G):\supp(f)\subseteq\G[0]\right\}.\] \end{definition} Since $\supp(fg)\subseteq\supp(f)\supp(g)$ and $\supp(f+\lambda g)\subseteq\supp(f)\cup\supp(g)$ for all $f,g\in A_R(\G)$ and $\lambda\in R$, it follows that $D_R(\G)$ is indeed a subalgebra of $A_R(\G)$. Moreover, the convolution product on $D_R(\G)$ coincides with the pointwise product: for all $f,g\in D_R(\G)$ and $x\in\G[0]$, we have $\G^x\cap\G[0]=\left\{x\right\}$, so \[(fg)(x)=\sum_{a\in\G^x\cap\supp(f)}f(a)g(a^{-1}x)=f(x)g(x^{-1}x)=f(x)g(x).\] In particular, $D_R(\G)$ is commutative, since we assume the ring $R$ is commutative. Moreover, $D_R(\G)$ is isomorphic to $A_R(\G[0])$ via the map \[E:D_R(\G)\to A_R(\G[0]),\qquad f\mapsto f|_{\G[0]}.\] whose inverse is given as follows: If $f\in A_R(\G[0])$, then $E^{-1}(f)$ is the extension of $f$ to $\G$ by setting $E^{-1}(f)=0$ on $\G\setminus\G[0]$. \subsection{Diagonal-preserving isomorphisms of Steinberg Algebras} The goal of this section is to prove that the Steinberg algebra of an ample Hausdorff groupoid $\G$ together with its diagonal algebra completely characterize $\G$. Although the main theorem of this subsection (Theorem \ref{theoremsteinbergalgebras}) is partially stated and proven (for more general \emph{graded} Steinberg algebras) in \cite[Corollary 3.14]{carlsenrout2017}, we can obtain a precise classification of the diagonal-preserving isomorphisms of Steinberg algebras, as described in Theorem \ref{theoremsteinbergalgebras} and Corollary \ref{corollaryclassificationofautomorphismsofsteinbergalgebras} We will need to recover the bisections of $\G$ from $A_R(\G)$, and in particular the compact-open subsets of $\G[0]$. The main idea is, again, to identify subsets of $\G[0]$ with their characteristic functions, and these are precisely the functions which attain only the values $0$ and $1$. We thus need to assume an extra condition on the ring $R$. \begin{definition}[{\cite[X.7]{MR1878556}}]\index{Indecomposable ring} A (nontrivial) commutative unital ring $R$ is \emph{indecomposable} if its only idempotents are $0$ and $1$. Equivalently, $R$ is indecomposable if it cannot be written as a direct sum $R\simeq R_1\oplus R_2$, where $R_1$ and $R_2$ are nontrivial rings. \end{definition} A subset $A$ of a groupoid $\G$ is a bisection if and only if $AA^{-1}\cup A^{-1}A\subseteq \G[0]$. A similar type of condition will be used to recover an ample Hausdorff groupoid $\G$ from the pair $(A_R(\G),D_R(\G))$. \begin{definition}\index{Normalizer (of the diagonal subalgebra)} A \emph{normalizer} of $D_R(\G)$ is an element $f\in A_R(\G)$ for which there exists $g\in A_R(\G)$ such that \begin{enumerate} \item[(i)] $fD_R(\G)g\subseteq D_R(\G)$ and $gD_R(\G)f\subseteq D_R(\G)$; \item[(ii)] $fgf=f$ and $gfg=g$. \end{enumerate} We denote by $N_R(\G)$ the set of normalizers of $D_R(\G)$. An element $g$ satisfying (i) and (ii) above will be called and \emph{inverse of $f$ relative to $D_R(\G)$}. \end{definition} \begin{example} If $A\in\KB(\G)$ then $1_A\in N_R(\G)$. More generally, if $\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n$ are invertible elements in $R$ and $U_1,\ldots,U_n$ are compatible disjoint compact-open bisections (that is, $\bigcup_i U_i$ is also a bisection), then $f=\sum_i \lambda_i1_{U_i}$ is a normalizer of $D_R(\G)$. The unique inverse of $f$ relative to $D_R(\G)$ is given by $f^*=\sum_i\lambda_i^{-1}1_{U_i^{-1}}$, that is, $f^*(a)=f(a^{-1})^{-1}$ for all $a\in\supp(f)^{-1}$. \end{example} In order to recover $\KB(\G)$ from $(A_R(\G),D_R(\G))$, we need that all normalizers of $D_R(\G)$ have the form described in the example above, so additional conditions will have to be assumed on the groupoids we consider. The next few results show us that $N_R(\G)$ encodes some of the semigroup structure of $\KB(\G)$. \begin{lemma}\label{lemmanormalizerssteinbergalgebradiagonal1} Suppose that $f\in N_R(\G)$. \begin{enumerate} \item[(a)] If $g$ is an inverse of $f$ relative to $D_R(\G)$, then $fg,gf\in D_R(\G)$. \item[(b)] The inverse of $f$ relative to $D_R(\G)$ is unique. \end{enumerate} \end{lemma} \begin{proof} \begin{enumerate} \item[(a)] We have $fg=f1_{\so(\supp(f))}g\in D_R(\G)$ and similarly $gf=g1_{\ra(\supp(f))}f\in D_R(\G)$. \item[(b)] Let $g_1,g_2$ be inverses of $f$ relative to $D_R(\G)$. Then \[g_1=g_1fg_1=g_1(fg_2f)g_1=(g_1f)(g_2f)g_1.\] But since $g_1f$ and $g_2f\in D_R(\G)$, which is a commutative subalgebra, then \[g_1=(g_1f)(g_2f)g_1=(g_2f)(g_1f)g_1=g_2fg_1=g_2(fg_2f)g_1=g_2(fg_2)(fg_1),\] and similarly, since $fg_2,fg_1\in D_R(\G)$, \[g_1=g_2(fg_2)(fg_1)=g_2(fg_1)fg_2=g_2(fg_1f)g_2=g_2fg_2=g_2.\qedhere\] \end{enumerate} \end{proof} \begin{definition} We denote the inverse of $f\in N_R(\G)$ relative to $D_R(\G)$ by $f^*$. \end{definition} \begin{proposition}\label{propositionNRGisinversesemigroup} $N_R(\G)$ is an inverse semigroup under products, and the inverse of $f\in N_R(\G)$ is precisely the inverse relative to $D_R(\G)$. \end{proposition} \begin{proof} Since $D_R(\G)$ is commutative, we can use Lemma \ref{lemmanormalizerssteinbergalgebradiagonal1}(a) for $f,g\in N_R(\G)$ to obtain \[(fg)(g^*f^*)(fg)=f(gg^*)(f^*f)g=f(f^*f)(gg^*)g=fg\] and similarly $(g^*f^*)(gf)(g^*f^*)=g^*f^*$. Moreover, \[(fg)D_R(\G)(g^*f^*)=f\left(gD_R(\G)g^*\right)f^*\subseteq fD_R(\G)f^*\subseteq D_R(\G)\] and similarly $(g^*f^*)D_R(\G)(fg)\subseteq D_R(\G)$. This means that $fg\in N_R(\G)$, with inverse relative to $D_R(\G)$ equal to $g^*f^*$. Therefore $N_R(\G)$ is a regular semigroup. We will prove that $N_R(\G)$ is an inverse semigroup by proving that its idempotents commute. Let $f\in E(N_R(\G))$, that is, $f^2=f$. Then since $ff^*,f^*f\in D_R(\G)$ (Lemma \ref{lemmanormalizerssteinbergalgebradiagonal1}(a)), \[f^*=f^*ff^*=(f^*f)(ff^*)=(ff^*)(f^*f)=f(ff^*)(f^*f)f=f(f^*f)(ff^*)f=f^2=f\] and thus $f=f^*f\in D_R(\G)$. This proves $E(N_R(\G))\subseteq D_R(\G)$, so idempotents in $N_R(\G)$ commute and $N_R(\G)$ is an inverse semigroup.\qedhere \end{proof} The following property was considered in \cite{arxiv1711.01903v2}, when working on the same recovery problem. \begin{definition}\label{definitionlocalbisectionhypothesis} If $\G$ is an ample Hausdorff groupoid and $R$ is an indecomposable (commutative, unital) ring, we say that $(\G,R)$ satisfies the \emph{local bisection hypothesis} if $\supp(f)$ is a bisection for all $f\in N_R(\G)$. \end{definition} The following stronger condition was considered in \cite{carlsenrout2017}, and is more easily checked than the one above.% For applications of this condition, see Corollaries \ref{} and \ref{} \begin{definition} If $\G$ is an ample Hausdorff groupoid and $R$ is an indecomposable (commutative, unital) ring, we say that $(\G,R)$ \emph{satisfies condition (S)} if the set of all $x\in\G[0]$ such that the group ring $R\G_x^x$ has only trivial units\ftnt{If $G$ is a group and $R$ is a unital ring, a trivial unit of $RG$ is an element of the form $ug$ where $u$ is invertible in $R$ and $g\in G$.} is dense in $\G[0]$.\nocite{MR3743184} \end{definition} The property of a group-ring $RG$ (where $G$ is a group and $R$ is a ring) having only trivial units has been studied, for example, in \cite{MR0002137}. A map $T:A_R(X) \rightarrow A_R(Y)$ is an isomorphism of $R$-algebras if and only if there exists a (necessarily unique) homeomorphism $\phi:Y\to X$ such that $T(f)=f\circ\phi$ for all $f\in A_R(X)$. \end{proposition} \begin{proof} The ``if'' part is straightforward, so suppose that$T:A_R(X)\to A_R(Y)$ is an isomorphism of $R$-algebras. First we describe the ideals associated with clopen sets algebraically. The following definition is an algebraic version of \cite[Definition 1.5.9]{MR2391387}. \begin{definition} A \emph{conditional expectation} of a $R$-algebra $A$ onto a subalgebra $B$ is an $R$-module map $E:A\to B$ such that \begin{enumerate} \item[(i)] $E(b)=b$ for all $b\in B$ (i.e., $E$ is a \emph{projection} onto $B$); \item[(ii)] For all $a\in A$ and $b\in B$, $E(ba)=bE(a)$ and $E(ab)=E(a)b$ (i.e., $E$ is a $B$-bimodule morphism). \end{enumerate} \end{definition} \begin{remark} If $B$ has local units then condition (ii) above can be substituted by \begin{enumerate} \item[(ii)'] For all $a\in A$ and $b,b'\in B$, $E(bab')=bE(a)b'$. \end{enumerate} Indeed, if $a\in A$ and $b\in B$, let $u\in B$ be a unit for $\left\{E(ab),b,E(a)\right\}$. Then \[E(ab)=uE(ab)u\overset{(ii)'}{=}E(uabu)=E(uab)=uE(a)b=E(a)b,\] and similarly $E(ba)=bE(a)$, so (ii) is satisfied. \end{remark} \begin{example} Let $R$ be any (nontrivial) commutative unital ring. We consider the subalgebras $B\subseteq A$ of the $2\times 2$ matrix algebra $M_2(R)$ \[A=\left\{\begin{bmatrix}a&0\\b&c\end{bmatrix}:a,b,c\in R\right\}\qquad\text{and}\qquad B=\left\{\begin{bmatrix}0&0\\a&0\end{bmatrix}:a\in R\right\}.\] Let $E:A\to B$ be given by $E\left(\begin{bmatrix}a&0\\b&c\end{bmatrix}\right)=\begin{bmatrix}0&0\\b&0\end{bmatrix}$. Then $E$ is a projection of $A$ onto $B$ and satisfies (ii)', because $BAB=0$. However, $E$ is not a left (nor right) $B$-module morphism, because \[\begin{bmatrix}0&0\\1&0\end{bmatrix}E\left(\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{bmatrix}\right)=0,\qquad\text{but}\qquad E\left(\begin{bmatrix}0&0\\1&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{bmatrix}\right)=\begin{bmatrix}0&0\\1&0\end{bmatrix}.\] \end{example} \begin{lemma}\label{lemmaidealsofclopens} Let $R$ be an indecomposable ring, $X$ a zero-dimensional, locally compact Hausdorff space and $U$ an open subset of $X$. Then the following are equivalent: \begin{enumerate} \item[(1)] $U$ is clopen; \item[(2)] There exists a conditional expectation of $A_R(X)$ onto $\mathbf{I}(U)$; \item[(3)] There exists an ideal $J$ of $A_R(X)$ such that $A_R(X)=\mathbf{I}(U)\oplus J$ (as $R$-modules). \end{enumerate} \end{lemma} \begin{proof} (1)$\Rightarrow$(2): If $U$ is clopen, then $1_U$ is continuous and the map $E:A_R(X)\to\mathbf{I}(U)$, $E(f)=f1_U$ is a conditional expectation. (Note that $E$ is well-defined even though $1_U$ does not belong to $\mathbf{I}(U)$ if $U$ is not compact.) (2)$\Rightarrow$(3): Suppose $E:A_R(X)\to\mathbf{I}(U)$ is a conditional expectation, and define $J=\left\{f-E(f):f\in A_R(X)\right\}$. Then $J$ is an $R$-submodule of $A_R(X)$ such that $A_R(X)=\mathbf{I}(U)\oplus J$, since $E$ is a projection onto $\mathbf{I}(U)$. Let us check that $J$ is a right ideal. Given $f,g\in A_R(X)$, let $u\in\mathbf{I}(U)$ be a local unit for $\left\{E(fg),E(f)g\right\}$. Since $E$ is a right $\mathbf{I}(U)$-morphism and $\mathbf{I}(U)$ is an ideal, we have \[E(fg)=E(fg)u=E(fgu)=E(f)gu=E(f)g,\ntag\label{equationconditionalexpectationismorphism}\] so \[(f-E(f))g=fg-E(f)g=fg-E(fg)\in J,\] and therefore $J$ is a right ideal. Similarly, $J$ is a left ideal. (3)$\Rightarrow$(1): Suppose that $A_R(X)=\mathbf{I}(U)\oplus J$ for some ideal $J$. Let us show that $J\subseteq\mathbf{I}(X\setminus\overline{U})$. Indeed, if $f\in J$ and $x\in\overline{U}$, then $f$ is constant on a neighbourhood $V$ of $x$. Choose $y\in U\cap V$, and $W$ a compact-open neighbourhood satisfying $y\in W\subseteq U\cap V$. Since $\mathbf{I}(U)$ and $J$ are complementary ideals, then $f1_W=0$ and in particular \[f(x)=f(y)=(f1_W)(y)=0.\] Therefore $J\subseteq\mathbf{I}(X\setminus\overline{U})$. We can now prove that $U$ is clopen. Given $x\in\overline{U}$, let $V$ be any compact-open neighbourhood of $x$. By hypothesis, we may write $1_V=f+g$ for some $f\in\mathbf{I}(U)$ and $g\in J\subseteq\mathbf{I}(X\setminus\overline{U})$, so $1=1_V(x)=f(x)+g(x)=f(x)$. In particular $x\in\supp(f)\subseteq U$. Therefore $U$ is clopen.\qedhere \end{proof} \begin{remark} The same argument as used in Equation (\ref{equationconditionalexpectationismorphism}) can be used to show that every conditional expectation $E:A\to I$ of an $R$-algebra $A$ onto an ideal $I$ with local units is actually an $R$-algebra morphism. \end{remark} By Lemma \ref{lemmaidealsofclopens} and Theorems \ref{prop:weaksemilattice} and \ref{theoremsteinbergiscrossed}, we conclude the following: \begin{theorem}\label{theoremcrossedproductasasteinbergalgebra} Let $S$ be an inverse semigroup which is a weak semilattice, $R$ be an indecomposable ring and $X$ a zero-dimensional, locally compact Hausdorff space. Let $\alpha=\left(\alpha_s:D_{s^*}\to D_s\right)_{s\in S}$ be an algebraic partial action of $S$ on $A_R(X)$ where each ideal $D_s$ has local units and satisfies any condition of Lemma \ref{lemmaidealsofclopens}. Then $A_R(X)\ltimes_{\alpha} X$ is isomorphic to a Steinberg algebra $A_R(S\ltimes_{\theta} X)$ where $\theta$ is a topological partial action of $S$ on $X$ which induces $\alpha$. \end{theorem}
