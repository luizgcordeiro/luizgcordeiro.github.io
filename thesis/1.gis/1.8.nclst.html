We need to assume one additional condition on such semigroups -- namely that the corresponding groupoid under non-commutative Stone duality is $\sigma$-compact. This condition also guarantees that the Borel structure of the groupoid is sufficiently manageable , and is always satisfied by unital Boolean algebras, so we obtain a true generalization of the classical Loomis-Sikorski theorem - which we briefly recall. \subsection{Classical Loomis-Sikorski Theorem} \begin{definition}[{\cite[314A]{MR2459668}}]\index{$\sigma$-complete!Boolean algebra} A generalized Boolean algebra $B$ is said to be \emph{$\sigma$-complete} if it admits joins of countable subsets. \end{definition} \begin{definition}[{\cite[322A]{MR2459668}}]\index{Probability algebra}\label{definitionprobabilityalgebra} A \emph{probability algebra} is a pair $(B,\mu)$, where $B$ is a $\sigma$-complete unital Boolean algebra and $\mu:B\to[0,1]$ is a function, called a \emph{probability measure}, satisfying: \begin{enumerate} \item[(i)] $\mu(0)=0$; \item[(ii)] $\mu(e)>0$ whenever $e\neq 0$; \item[(iii)] $\mu(1)=1$; \item[(iv)] If $\left\{e_n\right\}$ is a sequence of disjoint elements of $B$, then \[\mu\left(\bigvee_{n\in\mathbb{N}} a_n\right)=\sum_{n\in\mathbb{N}}\mu(a_n).\] \end{enumerate} In other terms, $\mu$ is a normalized, faithful invariant mean on $B$ (regarded as an inverse semigroup) satisfying the \emph{countable additivity} described in (iv) (as opposed to only finite additivity). \end{definition} Alternatively, conditions (i) and (ii) could be substituted by the single condition $\mu^{-1}(0)=\left\{0\right\}$. \begin{example}\index{Measure algebra} Let $X$ be a space endowed with a $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{I}$ an ideal of $\mathcal{A}$ closed under countable unions. Then $\mathcal{A}/\mathcal{I}$ is a $\sigma$-complete unital Boolean algebra. If $\mu$ is a probability measure defined on $\mathcal{A}$ and $\mathcal{I}$ is the ideal of null sets of $\mathcal{A}$, then $\mu$ factors to a probability measure on the quotient $\mathcal{A}/\mathcal{I}$. In this case we denote $\mathcal{A}/\mathcal{I}$ endowed with the invariant mean $\mu$ by $\MAlg(X,\mu)$, and call it the \emph{measure algebra} of $(X,\mu)$. \end{example} \begin{definition} An \emph{isomorphism} between two probability algebras $(B,\mu)$ and $(C,\nu)$ is a Boolean algebra isomorphism $\phi:B\to C$ such that $\nu(\phi(a))=\mu(a)$ for all $a\in B$. \end{definition} There are two versions of interest of the classical Loomis-Sikorski theorem, which read as follow: \begin{theorem}[\cite{MR0021084}] If $B$ is a $\sigma$-complete unital Boolean algebra, then there is a set $X$, a $\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{A}$ on $X$ and an ideal $\mathcal{I}$ of $\mathcal{A}$ closed under countable unions such that $B$ is isomorphic to $\mathcal{A}/\mathcal{I}$. \end{theorem} \begin{theorem}[\cite{MR0017736}] Every probability algebra $(B,\mu)$ is isomorphic to $\MAlg(X,\mu)$ for some probability space $X$. \end{theorem} In fact, the second version of the theorem follows from the first one, since if $\mu$ is a probability measure on $\mathcal{A}/\mathcal{I}$ (in the sense of Definition \ref{definitionprobabilityalgebra}), then $\mu$ induces a probability measure (in the usual sense) on $(X,\mathcal{A})$, via composition with the quotient map $\mathcal{A}\to\mathcal{A}/\mathcal{I}$, and $\mathcal{I}$ is the ideal of null sets. %Moreover, we can classify all probability algebras which are isomorphic to $\operatorname{MAlg}([0,1],\lambda)$, where $\lambda$ is the Lebesgue measure of $[0,1]$. %\begin{definition} %An \emph{atom} of a Boolean algebra $B$ (or of a probability algebra $(B,\mu)$) is a minimal element of $B\setminus\left\{0\right\}$. Theorem 1.8.17 (Non-commutative Loomis-Sikorski) Let $S$ be a $\sigma$-complete Boolean inverse semigroup satisfying (SC). Then there exists a $\ra$-discrete Borel groupoid $\mathcal{G}$ and an ideal $\mathcal{I}\subseteq\BOR(\mathcal{G})$ such that $S$ is isomorphic to $\BOR(\mathcal{G})/\mathcal{I}$.

\begin{proof} Using non-commutative Stone duality, more precisely Proposition \ref{propositionnaturaltransformationsncsd}, we let $\G=\GP(S)$, which is an ample Hausdorff groupoid, and $\sigma$-compact since $S$ satisfies (SC) (Proposition \ref{propositionamplescsigmacompact}). Endowing $\G$ with the Baire $\sigma$-algebra (generated by $\KB(\G)$) we obtain a Borel groupoid (Example \ref{examplesigmacompactampleisborel}). Moreover, $\BOR(\G)$ coincides with the set of bisections which are Borel subsets of $\G$ (Proposition \ref{propositionwheneveryborelbisectionisborelbisection}). Note that, even though $\KB(\G)\simeq S$ is $\sigma$-complete, countable joins in $\KB(\G)$ will not be given by unions in general, and similarly for meets. Let $\zeta=\zeta_S:S\to\KB(\G)\subseteq\BOR(\G)$ be the canonical isomorphism (Proposition \ref{propositionnaturaltransformationsncsd}). We fix a countable family $\left\{c_n\right\}_{n\in\mathbb{N}}$ in $S$ satisfying properties (SC1) and (SC2), and let $C_n=\zeta(c_n)$, so that $\G\subseteq\bigcup_n C_n$. Define \[\mathcal{N}=\left\{\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty\zeta(s_n):s_1,s_2,\ldots\in S\text{ and }\bigwedge_{n=1}^\infty s_n=0\right\}\] and let $\mathcal{I}$ be the collection of those $A\in\BOR(\G)$ which are contained in some countable union $\bigcup_{m=1}^\infty N_m$, where $N_m\in\mathcal{N}$. Then $\mathcal{I}$ is clearly closed under countable unions. To check it is an ideal, first suppose $K=\bigcap_n\zeta(s_n)\in\mathcal{N}$, where $\bigwedge_n s_n=0$. For $A\in\BOR(\G)$, \[AK\subseteq\bigcup_{m=1}^\infty C_m\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty\zeta(s_n)=\bigcup_{m=1}^\infty\bigcap_{n=1}^\infty\zeta(c_m s_n)\] and for all $m$, $\bigwedge_n c_m s_n=c_m\bigwedge s_n=0$, which proves that $AK\in\mathcal{I}$. Similarly, $KA\in\mathcal{I}$, so $\mathcal{I}$ is a $\sigma$-ideal. Let $\theta:S\to\BOR(\G)/\mathcal{I}$, $\theta(s)=\widetilde{\zeta(s)}^I$, the composition of $\zeta$ with the quotient map $\BOR(\G)\to\BOR(\G)/\mathcal{I}$. We will prove that $\theta$ is an isomorphism, and we do this in several steps, the first one being a weaker form of injectivity. \begin{description} \item[If $s\neq 0$ then $\zeta(s)\not\in\mathcal{I}$:] Indeed, suppose $N_1,N_2,\ldots\in\mathcal{N}$, where $N_n=\bigcap_m\zeta(a^n_m)$ ($a^m_n\in S$) are such that $\bigwedge_m a^n_m=0$ for each $n$ . Since $s\neq 0$ and \[0\neq s=s\setminus 0=s\setminus\bigwedge_m a^1_m=\bigvee_m(s\setminus a^1_m)\] then there exists a number $k(1)$ such that $s\setminus a^1_{k(1)}\neq 0$. By induction on $N$, we can find a sequence $k(1),k(2),\ldots$ such that $s\setminus(a^1_{k(1)}\lor\cdots\lor a^N_{k(N)})\neq 0$ for all $n$. This means that $\zeta(s)\setminus\bigcup_{n=1}^N\zeta(a^n_{k(n)})\neq\varnothing$ for all $N\in\mathbb{N}$, that is, $\zeta(s)$ is not contained in any finite union $\bigcup_{n=1}^N\zeta(a^n_{k(n)})$, $N\in\mathbb{N}$. Since $\zeta(s)$ is compact, Cantor's Intersection Theorem implies that $\zeta(s)$ is not contained in $\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty\zeta(a^n_{k(n)})$, and in particular $\zeta(s)$ is also not contained in $\bigcup_{n=1}^\infty N_n$. This proves that $\zeta(s)\not\in\mathcal{I}$ whenever $s\neq 0$. \item[$\theta$ is injective:] If $a\neq b$ in $S$, then either $a\setminus b$ or $b\setminus a\neq 0$, which implies that either $\zeta(a)\setminus\zeta(b)$ or $\zeta(b)\setminus\zeta(a)\not\in\mathcal{I}$, but in any case we obtain $\theta(a)\neq\theta(b)$, so $\theta$ is injective. \item[$\theta$ preserves countable joins:] If $s_1,s_2,\ldots\in S$ are compatible, then $\bigvee_{j=1}^\infty s_j$ is an upper bound of all $s_i$, and so $\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty\zeta(s_i)\subseteq\zeta\left(\bigvee_{j=1}^\infty s_j\right)$. Since \[\zeta\left(\bigvee_{j=1}^\infty s_j\right)\setminus\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty\zeta(s_i)=\bigcap_{i=1}^\infty\zeta\left(\bigvee_{j=1}^\infty s_j\setminus s_i\right)\] and \[\bigwedge_{i=1}^\infty\left(\bigvee_{j=1}^\infty s_j\setminus s_i\right)=\left(\bigvee_{j=1}^\infty s_j\right)\setminus\left(\bigvee_{i=1}^\infty s_i\right)=0,\] we conclude that $\zeta\left(\bigvee_{j=1}^\infty s_j\right)\setminus\bigcup_{i=1}^\infty\zeta(s_i)\in\mathcal{I}$ and we are done. \end{description} Consider again, the fixed family of elements $C_n=\zeta(c_n)$ with $\G=\bigcup_n C_n$, and let $p\in\mathbb{N}$ be temporarily fixed. In order to prove that $\theta$ is surjective we first prove a weaker version. Recall from Section \ref{sectionquotientsofsemigroups} that $A\sim_I B$ means that $\widetilde{A}^I=\widetilde{B}^I$. \begin{description} \item[If $A\subseteq C_p$ is Borel, then $A\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\zeta(s)$ for some $s\leq c_p$:] The collection of Borel subsets of $C_p$ is generated, as a $\sigma$-algebra, by the family \[\left\{A\in\KB(\G):A\subseteq C_p\right\}=\left\{\zeta(a):a\leq c_p\right\},\] so we need to prove that the collection $\mathcal{B}$ of those Borel $A\subseteq C_p$ which are $\mathcal{I}$-equivalent to some $\zeta(s)$, where $s\leq c_p$, forms a $\sigma$-algebra on $C_p$. If $A\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\zeta(s)$, then $C_p\setminus A\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\zeta(c_p\setminus s)$, so $\mathcal{B}$ is closed under complements (in $C_p$), If $A_1,A_2,\ldots\in\mathcal{B}$, choose $s_1,s_2,\ldots\leq c_p$ with $A_i\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\zeta(s_i)$. In particular the $s_i$ are pairwise compatible, and we have already verified that $\zeta(\bigvee_i s_i)$ is $\mathcal{I}$-equivalent to $\bigcup_i\zeta(s_i)$, and thus $\mathcal{I}$-equivalent to $\bigcup_i A_i$ as well (Theorem \ref{theoremquotientmapsissigma}) %\item[$E(\BOR(\G)/I)=\theta(E(S))$:] Every element of $E(\BOR(\G)/I)$ is the image of some $A\in E(\BOR(\G))$, so we decompose $A=\bigcup_n A_n$, where $A_n\subseteq C_n$, and then take $e_n\in S$ with $A_n\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\zeta(s_n)$. Substituting $e_n$ by $e_n^*e_n$ if necessary, we may assume $e_n\in E(S)$. This way we construct a compatible countable family $\left\{e_n\right\}_n$ in such a way that %\[\zeta\left(\bigvee_{n=1}^\infty e_n\right)\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\bigcup_n\zeta(e_n)\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\bigcup_n A_n=A\] %as we wanted. \item[$\theta$ is surjective:] Let $A\in\BOR(\G)$ be arbitrary. Again, decompose $A=\bigcup_n A_n$ where $A\subseteq C_n$. Choose $s_n\in S$ with $\zeta(s_n)\sim_{\mathcal{I}} A_n$. The $A_n$ are pairwise compatible, so $\theta(s_n)$ are also pairwise compatible. Since $\theta$ is injective%and its image contains $E(\mathcal{B}/\mathcal{I})$ , the $s_n$ are pairwise compatible. Again using the fact that $\theta$ preserves countable compatible joins, we see that $A\sim_{\mathcal{I}}\zeta(\bigvee_n s_n)$.\qedhere \end{description} \end{proof} We can thus obtain a version characterizing inverse Boolean monoids satisfying (SC) and endowed with countably additive invariant means.

Corollary 1.8.18 Suppose $S$ is a $\sigma$-complete Boolean inverse monoid satisfying (SC), endowed with an invariant mean $\mu$ which is countably additive (i.e., a probability on $E(S)$). Then there exists a $\ra$-discrete probability measure-preserving groupoid $(\mathcal{G},\mu')$ and an isomorphism from $S$ to $\MEAS(\mathcal{G},\mu')$ which is isometric with respect to the uniform metrics.
